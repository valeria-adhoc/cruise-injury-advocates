Understanding the Death on the High Seas Act

Few losses are harder to absorb than the death of someone you love during what was supposed to be a vacation or a working voyage. When a death happens far out at sea, the grief is compounded by confusion: families are often told that the ordinary rules of state wrongful-death law do not apply, that a foreign flag or a distant location changes everything, and that the cruise line's lawyers have already begun building a file. In that moment, understanding the law that governs a death on the water can feel impossible. This page is written to help you begin.

The Death on the High Seas Act, commonly abbreviated DOHSA and codified at 46 U.S.C. section 30301 and the sections that follow, is a federal statute that gives certain family members a right to recover when a person dies because of a wrongful act, neglect, or default occurring on the high seas. It was enacted in 1920 to fill a gap in American maritime law. Before DOHSA, a death that occurred beyond the reach of any state's waters could leave a grieving family with no clear remedy at all, because the general maritime law of the time did not recognize a cause of action for wrongful death in many circumstances. DOHSA created a uniform federal remedy so that families would not be left without recourse simply because their loved one died offshore rather than on land.

For the families of cruise passengers and for the families of crew members and seafarers, DOHSA is frequently the statute that decides what a claim looks like, who may bring it, and what can be recovered. It is also, as many courts and commentators have observed, one of the more restrictive wrongful-death statutes in American law. Because those restrictions can feel unfair to families who are grieving, it is important to understand both what the law provides and what it withholds, so that expectations are grounded in reality rather than in the broader remedies people associate with wrongful-death cases on land.

When DOHSA applies

DOHSA does not apply to every death connected to a cruise or a ship. It applies to a specific category of deaths defined by two elements: the nature of the wrong and the location where it occurred. First, the death must result from a wrongful act, neglect, or default. That language is broad enough to cover negligence, unseaworthiness of a vessel, and a range of other failures, but it does require that someone's fault contributed to the death; a purely natural death from causes no one could have prevented generally does not give rise to a DOHSA claim. Second, and this is the element that most often surprises families, the wrongful act must have occurred on the high seas beyond a specific distance from shore.

Because both elements must be present, the same tragic event can fall inside or outside DOHSA depending on facts that seem, at first, to have nothing to do with the merits of the case. A fall that begins in territorial waters but proves fatal only later, an illness that develops during a voyage, or an incident during a shore excursion may each raise difficult questions about which body of law governs. Sorting through those questions is one of the first things experienced maritime counsel does, because the answer shapes everything that follows.

The three-nautical-mile rule and why location is decisive

DOHSA applies when the death results from a wrongful act, neglect, or default occurring on the high seas more than three nautical miles from the shore of the United States. That three-nautical-mile line is the single most consequential fact in many cruise death cases, because it determines whether DOHSA governs at all. Inside three nautical miles, a death may be governed by the general maritime law or by state wrongful-death statutes, which frequently allow a much wider range of damages. Outside three nautical miles, DOHSA typically controls, and its narrower rules apply.

The reason this matters so much is that the difference is not merely procedural. Under many state wrongful-death laws and under the general maritime law as applied within territorial waters, a family may be able to recover for the emotional dimensions of their loss: the grief they carry, the companionship they will never again share, the guidance a parent would have given a child. Under DOHSA, as explained below, those categories of loss are generally not recoverable. So the location of the wrongful act can be the difference between a claim that compensates a family for the full human weight of their loss and a claim limited to financial calculations. A few nautical miles can change the entire character of a case.

Determining exactly where a wrongful act occurred is not always straightforward. A cruise ship moves constantly, and the relevant question is not simply where the ship was when the death was pronounced, but where the wrongful act, neglect, or default took place. If a hazard was created or a failure occurred while the vessel was beyond three nautical miles, DOHSA may apply even if consequences unfolded elsewhere. Navigational logs, the ship's position data, medical records, and the timeline of events all become important evidence. This is one of many reasons families should preserve records early and consult counsel who handle maritime matters, rather than assume the answer is obvious.

Who may bring a DOHSA claim

DOHSA does not allow every grieving relative to file a lawsuit in their own name. The statute requires that the claim be brought by the personal representative of the person who died. The personal representative is the individual appointed to administer the decedent's estate, sometimes called an executor or administrator depending on the jurisdiction and on whether the person left a will. That representative acts in a representative capacity, bringing the claim not for their own benefit alone but for the benefit of the family members the statute identifies.

This structure has practical consequences. If no personal representative has yet been appointed, one of the first steps in pursuing a DOHSA claim is opening an estate and having a representative appointed through the appropriate probate process. Family members who are eager to act may need to navigate that appointment before a lawsuit can properly be filed. An attorney can help identify who is best positioned to serve, coordinate with probate counsel where necessary, and make sure the claim is brought in the correct name so that a technical defect does not jeopardize an otherwise valid case.

Who can be a beneficiary

Although the personal representative brings the claim, the recovery is for the benefit of a defined group of family members. Under DOHSA, the claim is brought for the benefit of the decedent's spouse, parent, child, or dependent relative. These are the individuals whose losses the statute is designed to address. Not every relative qualifies, and the category of dependent relative in particular turns on facts about the relationship and the degree of financial dependence that existed.

Because DOHSA focuses on financial loss, the identity and circumstances of the beneficiaries matter a great deal. A surviving spouse and minor children who depended on the decedent's income present very different questions than an adult child who was financially independent. A parent who relied on a working adult child for support may have a claim where a parent who did not cannot show the same loss. Establishing who the beneficiaries are and documenting the nature of their dependence is a central part of building a DOHSA case, and it is work that should be approached with sensitivity to the family's circumstances.

What DOHSA does and does not allow you to recover

The defining feature of DOHSA, and the source of most of the criticism directed at it, is the narrow scope of the damages it permits. DOHSA limits recovery to pecuniary losses, meaning financial losses that can be measured in money. It does not compensate for the emotional and relational dimensions of a death that many people would consider the heart of their loss.

Damages that are recoverable

Under DOHSA, the beneficiaries may generally recover for financial losses tied to the death, including the following categories:

  • Lost financial support. The income and financial contributions the beneficiaries could reasonably have expected to receive from the decedent had they lived, projected over the period of that expected support.
  • The monetary value of lost services. The economic value of services the decedent would have provided, such as household work, care, or other contributions that would otherwise have to be replaced at a cost.
  • Loss of nurture, guidance, care, and instruction. In the case of a child who has lost a parent, many courts recognize the pecuniary value of the guidance and instruction a parent would have provided, valued in economic rather than emotional terms.
  • Funeral expenses. In many courts, funeral expenses that were actually paid by a dependent beneficiary are recoverable as a pecuniary loss.

Each of these categories requires proof. Lost support is typically established through evidence of the decedent's earnings, work history, and expected career, often with the help of economic experts who calculate the present value of future contributions. The value of lost services is documented through evidence of what the decedent actually did for the family. Because these are financial calculations, careful, well-supported evidence makes a meaningful difference in what a family is ultimately able to recover.

Damages that are not recoverable

DOHSA generally does not allow recovery for the non-financial losses that families most deeply feel. In particular, and subject to the interaction with other laws discussed below, DOHSA generally does not permit recovery for:

  • Grief, sorrow, and mental anguish suffered by the surviving family members.
  • Loss of society, companionship, comfort, and consortium, meaning the loss of the relationship itself.
  • The decedent's own pre-death pain and suffering.
  • Punitive damages, in most applications of the statute.

There is a narrow statutory exception that allows certain additional recovery in cases arising from commercial aviation accidents occurring beyond twelve nautical miles from shore. That exception was added by Congress after a specific category of disasters and applies to commercial aviation, not to cruise ship deaths. Families of cruise passengers and crew should understand that this exception does not open the door to non-pecuniary damages in a typical cruise case.

It is difficult to explain to a grieving family that the law will not compensate them for their grief or for the loss of the person's presence in their lives. Many people are surprised and hurt to learn that the emotional core of their loss is not something DOHSA recognizes. That reaction is entirely understandable. The purpose of explaining these limits clearly is not to diminish the loss but to make sure families understand the terrain before they make decisions, so that no one is caught off guard later by a rule they were never told about.

How DOHSA interacts with the Jones Act and with passenger claims

DOHSA rarely operates in isolation. Depending on whether the person who died was a crew member or a passenger, other bodies of law come into play, and the way they fit together can significantly affect a family's options.

For crew members and seafarers: the Jones Act

When the person who died was a member of a ship's crew, the Jones Act may be relevant alongside DOHSA. The Jones Act is a federal statute that provides seamen, and in the case of death their representatives, with a negligence remedy against the employer. A crew member's death on the high seas can therefore implicate both DOHSA and the Jones Act, and the interaction between them raises questions about which damages are available and under which theory. In some situations the two statutes overlap; in others they address different defendants or different aspects of the claim. The general maritime law doctrine of unseaworthiness may also be part of the picture for crew.

The result is that a crew death case can involve multiple overlapping legal theories, each with its own rules about liability and damages, and each with its own limits. Coordinating those theories so that a family pursues the strongest available combination, while respecting the limits DOHSA imposes, requires counsel who understand maritime employment law and death claims together. For families of seafarers, the choice of theory is not academic; it can shape both what must be proven and what can be recovered.

For passengers: the ticket contract

When the person who died was a passenger, the cruise ticket contract almost always matters. Cruise passenger tickets are not simple receipts; they are contracts, and they typically contain provisions that shorten the time to file, require early written notice of a claim, and dictate where a lawsuit must be brought. Many major cruise lines that sail from Florida require that claims be filed in federal court in Miami, through what is known as a forum-selection clause. These provisions have generally been enforced by courts, which means that even a family living far from Florida, or overseas, may find that their case belongs in Miami.

For a family already carrying an enormous loss, the ticket contract can feel like an unwelcome obstacle: a document no one read closely at the time of booking, now controlling deadlines and venue. But it is a reality of cruise litigation. The shortened notice periods in particular can be unforgiving, and missing them can jeopardize a claim regardless of its merits. This is a central reason to consult counsel promptly rather than waiting until the family feels ready, because the contract's clock may already be running.

The statute of limitations

DOHSA has its own statute of limitations. A DOHSA claim generally must be brought within three years of the date the cause of action accrued, which is typically the date of death. Three years may sound like ample time, but in cruise cases it can be misleading, for two reasons.

First, the ticket contract's own deadlines can be much shorter than the statutory three years and can require written notice long before a lawsuit is filed. Where a passenger death is involved, the contract's notice and filing provisions may effectively compress the timeline well below three years. Second, the practical work of preserving evidence, opening an estate, appointing a personal representative, and investigating what happened at sea takes time, and evidence aboard a ship does not wait. Surveillance footage may be overwritten, crew members rotate off the vessel and disperse across the world, and records can become harder to obtain as time passes. A family that waits until close to a deadline may find that the case is far harder to prove than it would have been earlier.

Because deadlines in maritime death cases can be complicated and shorter than they appear, no family should assume they have plenty of time. The safest course is to have counsel review the specific facts, the ticket contract, and the applicable deadlines as early as possible, so that nothing important is lost to the passage of time.

Why DOHSA cases are legally complex and often criticized as harsh

DOHSA has drawn sustained criticism over the years, and the criticism comes from a real place. Because the statute limits recovery to pecuniary losses, it can produce outcomes that strike families and observers as deeply unfair. Consider that under DOHSA the value of a claim depends heavily on the financial contributions the decedent was expected to make. A person whose family depended on their income may support a substantial claim, while the death of a retiree, a homemaker, or a child, whose economic contributions are harder to quantify, may yield far less under the statute even though the human loss is no less profound. Many people find it troubling that the law measures a life primarily by its financial output.

The complexity compounds the harshness. A DOHSA case can require sorting out where a wrongful act occurred relative to the three-nautical-mile line, whether DOHSA or some other law governs, who the proper personal representative and beneficiaries are, how multiple overlapping statutes interact for crew, and what the ticket contract requires for passengers. Each of these questions can be contested, and cruise lines are represented by experienced maritime defense counsel who understand exactly how these rules can be used to narrow or defeat a claim. For a family without guidance, the combination of restrictive damages and procedural complexity can feel like the law is stacked against them.

None of this means a claim is not worth pursuing. It means that pursuing one wisely requires clear eyes about what the law allows, careful attention to procedure, and thorough development of the financial evidence that DOHSA does recognize. Where the facts allow, it also means identifying every additional theory of recovery, such as the Jones Act for crew, that might expand what is available beyond DOHSA's baseline. The goal is to make the fullest use of the remedies the law does provide.

What families should do

In the aftermath of a death at sea, families are rarely in a position to think about litigation, and they should not have to. Still, a few practical steps can protect a family's options while they grieve. Where you are able, and only where you feel able, the following can help:

  • Preserve every document. Keep the ticket, booking confirmations, boarding materials, and any correspondence with the cruise line. These records often contain the contract terms that will govern a claim.
  • Keep records of communications. Note the dates and content of any conversations with the cruise line or its representatives, and keep any letters or emails you receive.
  • Save related records. Medical records, incident reports, and any photographs or accounts from other passengers or witnesses can be valuable, and they can become harder to obtain as time passes.
  • Be cautious with early settlement discussions. Cruise lines and their insurers may reach out soon after a death. It is reasonable to decline to give recorded statements or sign documents until you have spoken with counsel.
  • Seek guidance about the estate. Because a DOHSA claim is brought by a personal representative, understanding the estate process early can prevent delay later.
  • Consult maritime counsel promptly. Because deadlines can be short and the applicable law can be complicated, an early conversation with a lawyer who handles maritime death claims can preserve options that might otherwise be lost.

Above all, families should give themselves permission to move at a human pace. Reaching out to a lawyer early is not about rushing to litigate; it is about making sure that grief is not compounded, months later, by the discovery that a deadline passed or evidence disappeared. A brief, unhurried conversation can answer questions and take one source of uncertainty off a family's shoulders.

Frequently asked questions

What is the Death on the High Seas Act in simple terms?

DOHSA is a federal law that gives certain family members a way to seek financial compensation when a person dies because of a wrongful act, neglect, or default that occurs on the high seas more than three nautical miles from the shore of the United States. It is the statute that often governs deaths connected to cruises that happen well offshore. Its defining feature is that it limits recovery to financial, or pecuniary, losses.

Why does the three-nautical-mile line matter so much?

The three-nautical-mile line determines whether DOHSA applies. If the wrongful act occurred more than three nautical miles from shore, DOHSA generally controls, and its narrow rules on damages apply. If it occurred within that distance, other laws, such as the general maritime law or a state wrongful-death statute, may govern instead, and those laws frequently allow recovery for a wider range of losses, including emotional ones. Because of this, the location of the wrongful act can dramatically change what a family can recover.

Who is allowed to file a DOHSA claim?

A DOHSA claim must be filed by the personal representative of the person who died, meaning the executor or administrator of the estate. The representative brings the claim on behalf of the beneficiaries the statute identifies. If no personal representative has been appointed, that appointment is usually one of the first steps, and an attorney can help arrange it.

Which family members can benefit from a DOHSA claim?

DOHSA allows recovery for the benefit of the decedent's spouse, parent, child, or dependent relative. Whether a particular relative qualifies, especially in the case of a dependent relative, depends on the facts of the relationship and the degree of financial dependence. Because DOHSA focuses on financial loss, the circumstances of the beneficiaries are an important part of any claim.

Can we recover for our grief or for losing our loved one's companionship?

Generally, no. DOHSA limits recovery to financial losses and does not permit compensation for grief, mental anguish, or the loss of society and companionship, nor for the decedent's own pre-death pain and suffering. There is a narrow exception for certain commercial aviation deaths beyond twelve miles from shore, but it does not apply to cruise cases. This limitation is one of the main reasons DOHSA is often criticized as harsh, and it is important that families understand it from the start.

What kinds of losses can we actually recover under DOHSA?

DOHSA allows recovery for pecuniary, or financial, losses. These commonly include the lost financial support the beneficiaries could have expected from the decedent, the monetary value of services the decedent would have provided, and, in the case of a child, the economic value of a parent's guidance and instruction. In many courts, funeral expenses paid by a dependent are also recoverable. Proving these losses often involves financial records and, in many cases, economic experts.

My loved one was a crew member. Does that change things?

It can. When the person who died was a member of the crew, the Jones Act and the general maritime law may apply alongside DOHSA. The Jones Act provides a negligence remedy against the employer, and unseaworthiness may also be relevant. These overlapping theories can affect what must be proven and what may be recovered, so crew death cases often require evaluating several bodies of law together to identify the strongest combination available.

Why do so many cruise cases end up in Miami?

Many cruise lines that operate out of Florida include forum-selection clauses in their ticket contracts requiring that claims be brought in federal court in Miami. Courts have generally enforced these clauses. As a result, families across the country and around the world may find that their case belongs in Miami. Because our firm is based there, we are positioned to represent families wherever they live.

How long do we have to file a DOHSA claim?

DOHSA generally has a three-year statute of limitations, usually running from the date of death. However, the cruise ticket contract may impose much shorter deadlines and early written-notice requirements for passenger claims, and the practical work of preserving evidence and opening an estate takes time. For all of these reasons, families should not assume they have plenty of time and should have the specific deadlines reviewed as early as possible.
