Maintenance and cure: an overview for injured and ill crew

If you work aboard a cruise ship, a ferry, a cargo vessel, or almost any other commercial vessel, and you are injured or become ill while in the service of that ship, the law gives you a set of protections that reaches back centuries. The most immediate of these is called maintenance and cure. It is one of the oldest remedies in maritime law, recognized long before modern workers' compensation systems existed, and it continues to protect seafarers today. Understanding what maintenance and cure is, when it applies, what it pays for, and how a shipowner can lawfully (and unlawfully) resist it can make an enormous difference to a crew member who is suddenly unable to work and far from home.

At its core, maintenance and cure is a promise. A vessel that puts a crew member to work at sea takes on a corresponding duty: if that person is hurt or falls ill during the period of employment, the shipowner must provide basic living expenses and reasonable medical care until the seaman recovers as fully as medicine allows. This duty exists independently of any lawsuit for negligence. A crew member does not have to prove that anyone did anything wrong to be entitled to maintenance and cure. That single feature, its no-fault character, is what makes it such an important and immediate source of support after an injury at sea.

What "maintenance" covers

The word "maintenance" is a term of art. It does not refer to the upkeep of the ship. Instead, it refers to a daily living allowance paid to a seaman who is recovering ashore and is unable to work. The idea is simple and humane: while you were aboard the vessel, the shipowner fed you and gave you a place to sleep. When an injury or illness forces you off the ship to recover, the owner should continue to cover the reasonable cost of the food and lodging you would otherwise have received on board. Maintenance is meant to keep a recovering seaman housed and fed, not to enrich anyone.

Maintenance is typically expressed as a daily rate. It is intended to reimburse the reasonable, actual cost of two basic categories of living expense:

  • Lodging. The reasonable cost of a place to live while you recover, comparable in a basic sense to the room and board provided aboard the vessel. This can include rent, and it often reaches related and necessary housing costs.
  • Food. The reasonable cost of meals during the recovery period, again measured against what would have been provided on the ship.

One of the most common and frustrating disputes in this area is the amount of the daily maintenance rate. Some shipowners and their insurers try to pay a very low, flat daily figure that has little relationship to what it actually costs a person to live in the real world. Courts have made clear that maintenance is supposed to reflect the reasonable, actual expenses a seaman incurs for food and lodging in the place where the seaman is recovering. Where a shipowner insists on an unrealistically low number, a crew member may be entitled to more, and documenting your real expenses, such as rent, utilities tied to your housing, and grocery costs, becomes important evidence.

Maintenance is a subsistence benefit. It is not designed to replace your full wages, cover entertainment, or pay for luxuries. But it is also not meant to be a token payment. The correct measure is what it reasonably costs the individual seaman to secure food and lodging comparable to what the vessel provided, and the law resolves genuine doubts about that amount in the seaman's favor.

What "cure" covers

"Cure" is the medical component of the remedy. It requires the shipowner to pay for the reasonable and necessary medical care a seaman needs to treat an injury or illness that arose in the service of the ship. Despite the word "cure," it does not mean the seaman must be fully healed or restored to perfect health. It means the shipowner pays for appropriate treatment until the seaman reaches the point where further care will no longer improve the condition.

Cure generally includes the reasonable cost of care such as:

  • Physician and surgeon visits, examinations, and consultations with specialists;
  • Hospitalization and emergency treatment;
  • Diagnostic testing, including imaging such as X-rays and MRIs;
  • Surgery and post-surgical follow-up;
  • Prescription medication;
  • Physical therapy, rehabilitation, and other prescribed therapeutic care;
  • Necessary medical devices, mobility aids, and related supplies.

Importantly, cure covers the reasonable and necessary costs of treatment; it is not a windfall for elective or unrelated care. But where treatment is genuinely needed to address a condition that arose in the service of the vessel, the shipowner's obligation to pay for that care is broad. Crew members sometimes assume they must accept whatever care the ship's doctor or a company-selected physician recommends. In fact, disputes frequently arise precisely because a crew member's own treating physician recommends continued care while the company's chosen doctor declares that no further treatment is needed. Which medical opinion controls is often at the heart of a maintenance and cure fight.

A no-fault remedy: whose fault the injury was usually does not matter

The single most important thing to understand about maintenance and cure is that it is a no-fault remedy. To recover it, a seaman does not have to prove that the shipowner, a supervisor, a co-worker, or anyone else was negligent. The obligation is triggered simply by the fact that the seaman became injured or ill while in the service of the ship. This is fundamentally different from a negligence lawsuit, where fault is the whole battle.

Because it is no-fault, maintenance and cure is available even in situations where no one did anything wrong at all. A crew member who develops appendicitis at sea, suffers a heart attack during a contract, or is hurt in an ordinary accident that no one could have prevented is still generally entitled to maintenance and cure. It does not even matter, in most circumstances, if the seaman's own carelessness contributed to the injury. The remedy exists to protect seafarers who, by the nature of their work, live and labor far from home and from the ordinary medical and social safety nets available on land.

There are narrow limits. Maintenance and cure can be denied where an injury results from the seaman's own willful misbehavior, gross misconduct, or intoxication, or where a seaman deliberately concealed a serious pre-existing medical condition when hired (discussed below in connection with the McCorpen defense). But outside of those defined exceptions, ordinary negligence by the seaman is not a defense, and the shipowner cannot escape the obligation by pointing fingers.

The law's protective attitude toward seafarers runs even deeper. Because seamen have historically been considered "wards of the admiralty," courts apply a longstanding rule: when there is genuine ambiguity or doubt about whether maintenance and cure is owed, or about its scope, that doubt is resolved in favor of the seaman. Shipowners do not get the benefit of close calls. This principle shapes nearly every maintenance and cure dispute.

"In the service of the ship": when the duty is triggered

The duty of maintenance and cure attaches when a seaman is injured or falls ill while "in the service of the ship." This phrase is interpreted broadly. It is not limited to moments when a crew member is actively performing job duties on deck. A seaman is generally considered in the service of the ship throughout the period of employment, including while subject to the call of duty, and often even while ashore on authorized leave during a contract.

This broad interpretation reflects the reality of a seafarer's life. Crew members live where they work. They are answerable to the vessel around the clock during a contract, and their time ashore during a voyage is frequently a limited break within an ongoing period of service. Because of this, injuries and illnesses that occur in a range of circumstances, not only while a crew member is on the clock, can fall within the scope of maintenance and cure. Whether a particular injury occurred "in the service of the ship" is a fact-specific question, and it is one that shipowners sometimes contest to avoid paying.

When the duty ends: maximum medical improvement

The shipowner's duty to pay maintenance and cure does not last forever. It continues until the seaman reaches what maritime law calls "maximum medical improvement," sometimes referred to as maximum cure. This is the point at which the seaman's condition has stabilized and further medical treatment is not reasonably expected to improve it. When a treating physician determines that additional care will only be palliative, meant to manage symptoms rather than to cure or improve the underlying condition, maximum medical improvement has generally been reached, and the ongoing obligation to pay cure and maintenance ends.

Two points about maximum medical improvement deserve emphasis. First, reaching maximum medical improvement does not mean the seaman is healthy or fully recovered. A crew member can reach maximum medical improvement while still living with a permanent disability or lasting pain. The standard is about whether further treatment will meaningfully improve the condition, not whether the seaman has returned to normal.

Second, maximum medical improvement is a medical determination, and it is frequently disputed. Shipowners have a strong financial incentive to declare that a seaman has reached maximum medical improvement as early as possible, because that declaration cuts off their obligation to keep paying. A company-retained physician may pronounce a crew member at maximum medical improvement while the crew member's own doctor believes additional surgery, therapy, or other treatment could still help. Because doubts are resolved in the seaman's favor, a premature or poorly supported declaration of maximum medical improvement is one of the most common issues our attorneys challenge on behalf of crew.

Unearned wages

Beyond maintenance and cure, an injured or ill seaman is often entitled to a third category of benefit: unearned wages. When a crew member is unable to complete a contract or voyage because of an injury or illness that arose in the service of the ship, the shipowner generally must pay the wages the seaman would have earned through the end of the contract period or voyage, as determined by the seaman's articles or employment agreement. These are the wages the seaman was already entitled to earn but could not because the injury or illness cut the service short.

Unearned wages can include not only base pay but, depending on the employment terms, other forms of compensation the seaman would have received, such as certain tips, gratuities, or overtime that were a regular part of the seaman's earnings. Because cruise ship crew often depend heavily on gratuities and shipboard bonuses, disputes over what counts as "wages" for this purpose can be significant. As with the rest of the remedy, the specific terms of the seafarer's contract and the applicable law shape what is owed, which is one more reason to have the employment agreement reviewed carefully.

How maintenance and cure fits with the Jones Act and unseaworthiness

Maintenance and cure is not the only remedy available to an injured seaman, and it is important to understand how it fits alongside the others. A crew member injured at sea may have as many as three distinct avenues of recovery, and they are not mutually exclusive:

  • Maintenance and cure. The no-fault remedy described here, providing living expenses and medical care regardless of who was at fault, until maximum medical improvement.
  • The Jones Act. A federal statute that allows a seaman to sue the employer for negligence. Unlike maintenance and cure, a Jones Act claim requires proving fault, but it also allows recovery of a fuller range of damages, including lost earning capacity, pain and suffering, and other losses that maintenance and cure does not cover.
  • Unseaworthiness. A separate claim, based on the shipowner's duty to provide a vessel that is reasonably fit for its intended purpose, including a competent crew, safe equipment, and safe working conditions. Unseaworthiness does not require proof of negligence in the ordinary sense; it focuses on whether the vessel or its appurtenances were defective or unsafe.

These remedies work together. A single injury can give rise to a maintenance and cure obligation, a Jones Act negligence claim, and an unseaworthiness claim all at once. Maintenance and cure typically provides immediate support while the seaman recovers, without waiting for the outcome of a lawsuit. The Jones Act and unseaworthiness claims, by contrast, are where a seaman can pursue the broader damages that maintenance and cure alone does not provide. Coordinating these claims strategically, so that pursuing one does not undermine another, is a central part of representing an injured crew member well.

Common disputes and defenses

Although maintenance and cure is a no-fault remedy that is supposed to be paid promptly, shipowners and their insurers frequently resist, delay, or minimize it. Understanding the tactics that come up most often helps crew members recognize when their rights are being shortchanged.

Cutting off benefits too early

Perhaps the most common dispute involves a shipowner declaring that the seaman has reached maximum medical improvement, and cutting off maintenance and cure, before that is actually true. This often happens when a company-selected physician issues an opinion that treatment is complete, even though the crew member is still symptomatic and the crew member's own doctor recommends further care. Because the shipowner's payment obligation ends at maximum medical improvement, an early declaration directly serves the company's financial interest.

Disputing whether a condition is still curable

Related to the timing issue, shipowners sometimes argue that a seaman's ongoing treatment is merely palliative, that is, aimed only at managing symptoms rather than improving the condition, and therefore that they no longer owe cure. Whether additional care is genuinely curative or merely palliative can be a close medical question, and it is exactly the kind of question that the law says should be resolved in the seaman's favor when there is real doubt.

Underpaying the maintenance rate

As noted above, some shipowners try to pay a low, flat daily maintenance rate that does not reflect the real cost of food and lodging. Crew members who accept such a rate without question may be receiving far less than they are entitled to. Documenting actual living expenses is often the key to correcting an inadequate rate.

The McCorpen concealment defense

One of the most important defenses shipowners raise is the concealment defense, widely known by the name of the case that shaped it, McCorpen. Under this defense, a shipowner argues that it does not owe maintenance and cure because the seaman intentionally concealed or misrepresented a pre-existing medical condition when applying for the job, typically on a pre-hire medical questionnaire or during a pre-employment medical examination.

To succeed with this defense, a shipowner generally must establish three things: first, that the seaman intentionally concealed or misrepresented medical information; second, that the concealed information was material to the employer's hiring decision; and third, that there is a connection between the concealed pre-existing condition and the injury or illness for which the seaman now seeks maintenance and cure. If the employer proves all three elements, it may be able to avoid the obligation entirely.

The concealment defense is heavily litigated, and it is not the automatic bar that shipowners sometimes suggest. The defense turns on intentional concealment. An honest mistake, a condition the seaman genuinely did not know about, a question that was ambiguous or poorly worded, or a prior issue that was fully resolved and unrelated to the current injury may not support the defense. The connection element also matters: a concealed condition affecting one part of the body may have nothing to do with an entirely different current injury. Our attorneys examine the pre-hire paperwork, the questions actually asked, the seaman's medical history, and the relationship (or lack of one) between any prior condition and the present injury to meet this defense head-on.

Contract, arbitration, and choice-of-law provisions

Seafarer employment agreements are often complex, and they frequently contain provisions that can dramatically affect how and where a claim is handled. Many crew contracts include arbitration clauses that require disputes to be resolved through private arbitration rather than in court, and some specify that the law of a particular foreign country will govern the seaman's claims. These provisions can influence the value of a claim, the procedures that apply, and even which remedies are available. Because these clauses can be enforced in some circumstances and challenged in others, having the contract reviewed early, before any deadline passes or any document is signed, is critically important.

Penalties when a shipowner wrongfully denies or delays

The law does not treat the duty of maintenance and cure as a mere suggestion. When a shipowner fails to pay maintenance and cure that is owed, there are escalating consequences depending on how the owner behaves.

If a shipowner is found simply to have wrongfully withheld maintenance and cure, it will of course owe the amounts that should have been paid. But if the shipowner's conduct in denying or delaying benefits is unreasonable, it can be liable for additional compensatory damages, meaning damages for the harm caused by the wrongful denial, such as a worsening of the seaman's medical condition that better care would have prevented.

The consequences climb higher still when the shipowner's conduct is not merely unreasonable but arbitrary, willful, callous, or in bad faith. In that situation, a shipowner can be exposed to attorney's fees and, significantly, to punitive damages. The United States Supreme Court, in Atlantic Sounding Co. v. Townsend, confirmed that punitive damages are available for the willful and wanton failure to pay maintenance and cure. This is a powerful protection: it means a shipowner that cynically stonewalls an injured crew member, denies clearly owed benefits, or drags out payment to pressure a desperate seaman may face damages well beyond the maintenance and cure it tried to avoid. The prospect of punitive damages and attorney's fees is often a meaningful source of leverage in negotiations with a resistant employer or insurer.

What a crew member should do after an injury or illness

The steps a crew member takes in the days and weeks after an injury or illness can significantly affect a maintenance and cure claim. While every situation is different, the following general practices tend to protect a seaman's rights:

  • Report the injury or illness promptly. Notify a supervisor or the appropriate officer and make sure the incident is recorded in the ship's records. Timely reporting helps establish that the condition arose in the service of the ship.
  • Seek medical attention and follow through with care. Get evaluated, follow the treatment recommendations of your own treating physicians, and keep going to appointments. Gaps in treatment can be used against you.
  • Keep copies of everything. Preserve incident reports, medical records, prescriptions, your employment contract, pay records, and any communications with the company about your injury and your benefits.
  • Document your living expenses. Keep records of your rent, housing costs, and food expenses during recovery. These support a proper maintenance rate.
  • Be careful about statements and paperwork. Be cautious before giving recorded statements, signing releases, or accepting a lump-sum "settlement" of your maintenance and cure. Once signed, such documents can be difficult to undo.
  • Do not assume the company's doctor has the last word. If a company-selected physician says you are fully recovered but you are still in pain or still symptomatic, that opinion can be challenged, especially when your own treating doctor disagrees.
  • Get legal advice early. Because contracts contain deadlines, arbitration clauses, and choice-of-law provisions, and because early decisions can shape the entire case, it is wise to consult a maritime attorney as soon as possible.

Frequently asked questions about maintenance and cure

Do I have to prove someone was at fault to receive maintenance and cure?

No. Maintenance and cure is a no-fault remedy. As long as you were injured or became ill while in the service of the vessel, you are generally entitled to it regardless of who, if anyone, was to blame. This is one of the key differences between maintenance and cure and a negligence claim under the Jones Act, which does require proof of fault.

How much is maintenance supposed to be each day?

Maintenance is meant to reimburse the reasonable, actual cost of the food and lodging you need while recovering ashore, comparable to what the vessel provided. There is no single universal figure; the correct amount depends on your real expenses in the place where you are recovering. Some shipowners try to pay a low flat rate, but if that rate does not reflect your actual reasonable costs, you may be entitled to more. Keeping records of your rent, housing costs, and food expenses helps establish the proper rate.

What exactly does "cure" pay for?

Cure covers the reasonable and necessary medical expenses to treat your injury or illness, including doctor and specialist visits, hospitalization, diagnostic testing, surgery, prescription medication, physical therapy and rehabilitation, and necessary medical devices. It continues until you reach maximum medical improvement, the point at which further treatment will not improve your condition.

When does the shipowner's obligation end?

The obligation to pay maintenance and cure continues until you reach maximum medical improvement, sometimes called maximum cure. That is the point at which additional medical treatment is no longer reasonably expected to improve your condition. Reaching maximum medical improvement does not mean you are fully healed; you can reach it while still living with a permanent injury. Because shipowners often declare maximum medical improvement too early, this determination is frequently disputed.

What is the McCorpen defense, and can it defeat my claim?

The McCorpen concealment defense is an argument that you are not entitled to maintenance and cure because you intentionally concealed or misrepresented a pre-existing medical condition when you were hired. To succeed, the shipowner generally must show that you intentionally concealed material medical information and that the concealed condition is connected to your current injury. The defense does not apply to honest mistakes, conditions you did not know about, or prior issues unrelated to your current injury. It is heavily litigated, and it can often be challenged successfully.

Can I receive maintenance and cure and also sue for my injury?

Yes. Maintenance and cure is separate from, and can be pursued alongside, a Jones Act negligence claim and an unseaworthiness claim. Maintenance and cure provides immediate no-fault support for living expenses and medical care, while the Jones Act and unseaworthiness claims allow you to seek broader damages, such as lost earning capacity and pain and suffering, if fault or an unsafe vessel condition can be shown. These claims are often pursued together.

What happens if the shipowner refuses to pay or delays my benefits?

If a shipowner wrongfully withholds maintenance and cure, it owes what should have been paid. If its conduct is unreasonable, it can owe additional compensatory damages. And if its denial or delay is arbitrary, willful, callous, or in bad faith, it can be liable for attorney's fees and even punitive damages, as the Supreme Court confirmed in Atlantic Sounding Co. v. Townsend. In other words, a shipowner that stonewalls an injured crew member can face consequences well beyond the benefits it tried to avoid paying.

My contract has an arbitration clause and mentions foreign law. Does that matter?

It can matter a great deal. Many seafarer employment agreements require disputes to be arbitrated rather than litigated in court, and some specify that the law of a particular foreign country will apply. These provisions can affect the procedures, the available remedies, and the value of your claim. They can sometimes be enforced and sometimes challenged. Because so much can turn on these clauses, it is important to have your contract reviewed by a maritime attorney early, before you sign anything or let a deadline pass.

The ship's doctor says I am fine, but I am still in pain. What can I do?

A company-selected physician's opinion is not necessarily the last word. If your own treating doctor believes you need further care or that you have not reached maximum medical improvement, that conflict can be challenged. Because doubts in maintenance and cure are resolved in the seaman's favor, a premature or poorly supported determination that you are fully recovered is a common and often successful point of dispute. Continuing to follow your own doctor's recommendations and keeping thorough records helps protect your claim.

I am not a U.S. citizen and I was working on a foreign-flagged ship. Can you still help me?

Possibly, yes. Many cruise ship crew members are not U.S. citizens and serve on foreign-flagged vessels, yet their disputes are frequently connected to Florida because that is where the cruise lines operate and where many of these cases are decided. We are based in Miami and represent crew members nationwide and worldwide. Whether and how U.S. maritime remedies apply to your situation depends on the specific facts and your contract, which is exactly why an early consultation is valuable.

How soon should I contact a lawyer?

As soon as you reasonably can. Employment contracts often contain short deadlines, notice requirements, arbitration clauses, and choice-of-law provisions, and early decisions, about medical care, statements, and paperwork, can shape your entire case. Speaking with a maritime attorney promptly helps ensure that evidence is preserved, deadlines are met, and you do not unknowingly give up rights.
